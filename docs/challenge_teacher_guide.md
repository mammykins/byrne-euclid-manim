# The Vault of the Last Geometer — Companion Teacher Guide

Answer keys and success criteria for `docs/challenge_worksheet.md`.

## Purpose

This guide sits beside the student worksheet and the Playlist 3 construction videos. It gives a quick marking route for each chamber, while keeping the mathematical reasoning visible rather than treating the constructions as drawing tricks.

Use it to:

- check whether a construction is genuinely correct
- listen for the key Euclidean idea in student explanations
- decide whether a pupil is ready to move to the next chamber

## Expedition framing

Run this as if the class is inside the vault with Professor Helena Vale.

The mood wants dust, echoes, bronze mechanisms, and a race against sunset. Keep the delivery brisk. Keep the stakes alive. The harbour still needs saving.

You do not need a full performance. A short cue before each chamber is enough to make the room feel like an expedition briefing rather than a detached worksheet.

Helpful habits:

- read the chamber cue aloud before playing the video
- award the chamber seal as soon as the construction and reasoning are both secure
- treat mistakes as the vault rejecting guesswork, not the pupil failing
- keep the rival treasure hunter in the background as a source of pressure

## Before you begin

- Accept different point labels if the reasoning is equivalent.
- Accept clean variant layouts if the construction is mathematically sound.
- Insist that construction arcs remain visible. They are evidence, not mess.
- Reward explanation. The final picture alone is not enough.

## Whole-vault success habits

Across the full sequence, a secure student usually:

- uses ruler and compasses rather than measuring by eye
- labels key points clearly
- leaves enough construction marks to show how the result was obtained
- explains equality, midpoint, or perpendicularity using the construction itself

## Chamber 1 — The Triangle Gate

**Read-aloud cue**

*The entrance slab groans but does not open. Three bronze sockets sit in the stone, two already fixed at points `A` and `B`. Professor Vale brushes away the dust and says, “The gate wants perfect equality. Build the third point cleanly, or it will seal us out.”*

**When the chamber yields**

*A low click travels through the wall. A triangular seal flashes red-gold, then drops into place. The first lock is satisfied.*

**Worksheet mission**

- `PropI` — construct an equilateral triangle on a given line

**What students should produce**

Given line segment `AB`, students construct a point on the intersection of two circles or arcs, one centred at `A` and one centred at `B`, each with radius `AB`, then join that point to `A` and `B`.

**Answer key**

1. **Why is the new point the same distance from `A` and `B`?**
   Because the chosen point lies on a circle centred at `A` with radius `AB`, and also on a circle centred at `B` with radius `BA`. That makes it equally distant from both endpoints.
2. **How do you know all three sides of the triangle are equal?**
   If the new point is `C`, then `AC = AB` from the first circle and `BC = BA` from the second. Since both equal `AB`, all three sides are equal, so `ABC` is equilateral.
3. **Which parts of the construction are essential, and which are only scaffolding?**
   The given segment, the two circles or arcs, their intersection, and the final joins are essential during the construction. Once the triangle is established, the arcs become scaffolding that shows how the result was justified.

**Success criteria**

- Student draws two valid arcs or circles from the endpoints of the given segment.
- Student identifies a correct intersection point and joins it to both endpoints.
- Student explains that equal radii create equal side lengths.

**Watch for**

- Pupils choosing a point that is not at the arc intersection.
- Pupils erasing the arcs too early.
- Pupils saying the triangle is equilateral because it “looks equal”.

## Chamber 2 — The Bridge of Equal Steps

**Read-aloud cue**

*Beyond the gate, a bridge crosses a floor of pressure tiles. A bronze stride-rod hangs from a chain beside the gap. “Match its length exactly from our anchor point,” says Vale. “One bad step and the whole causeway wakes up.”*

**When the chamber yields**

*Stone plates slide into alignment. The bridge settles with a harsh scrape, leaving a single safe path across the chamber.*

**Worksheet mission**

- `PropII` — copy a line segment from a given point

**What students should produce**

Starting from a given point such as `P`, students construct a new segment equal in length to a separate given segment such as `CD`, using construction rather than measurement.

**Answer key**

1. **Why can’t you simply measure `CD` with a ruler and mark it off?**
   Because the construction challenge is Euclidean: the straightedge draws lines but does not act as a measuring ruler. The equal length must be created geometrically.
2. **Which earlier construction idea helps make this one possible?**
   The equilateral-triangle idea from Proposition I helps. More broadly, students reuse the idea that circles or arcs carry exact equal distances.
3. **How can you justify that your new segment is exactly equal to `CD`?**
   The construction transfers the length by compass radius, supported by the auxiliary steps in Proposition II. The final segment is equal to `CD` because it is built from the same preserved distance, not estimated.

**Success criteria**

- Student begins from the given starting point rather than from the original segment.
- Student uses arcs or circles to transfer length, with visible construction marks.
- Student states clearly that equality comes from the preserved radius, not from measurement.

**Watch for**

- Pupils directly measuring with a numbered ruler.
- Pupils copying the segment in the wrong place.
- Pupils claiming equality without referring to the construction.

## Chamber 3 — The Counterweight Cut

**Read-aloud cue**

*A suspended gate blocks the next corridor. Its counterweight hangs on a frayed rope beside a carved key-length on the wall. Vale studies the groove and mutters, “Too long and the gate jams. Too short and the whole mechanism snaps.”*

**When the chamber yields**

*The rope catches at exactly the right point. The counterweight shifts. Somewhere above, old gears begin to turn again.*

**Worksheet mission**

- `PropIII` — cut off a segment equal to a shorter line

**What students should produce**

From a longer line such as `AB`, students mark a point so that one part of the longer line is exactly equal to a shorter given segment such as `CD`.

**Answer key**

1. **Where does the equal copied length appear in your finished construction?**
   It appears between one endpoint of the longer line and the newly marked cut point. If that cut point is labelled `E`, then the intended result is `AE = CD`.
2. **Why does this proposition depend on being able to copy a segment first?**
   Because the shorter length has to be transferred accurately onto the longer line. Proposition III relies on the segment-copying idea from Proposition II.
3. **What would go wrong if the marked point were placed by eye?**
   There would be no proof that the cut-off part matched the shorter segment. The construction would become a guess.

**Success criteria**

- Student selects one endpoint of the longer segment and marks off an equal part by construction.
- Student keeps the transferred-length evidence visible.
- Student explains that the final cut section is equal to the shorter line because it was copied, not estimated.

**Watch for**

- Pupils cutting from an arbitrary interior point.
- Pupils mixing up which segment is the source length and which is the target.
- Pupils giving a neat final picture with no construction evidence.

## Chamber 4 — The Mirror Chamber

**Read-aloud cue**

*Sunlight spears through a crack in the roof and ricochets wildly between polished mirrors. “Split the beam exactly,” says Vale, shielding her eyes. “Half a degree astray and we light the trap instead of the passage.”*

**When the chamber yields**

*The reflected beam steadies. A hidden panel glows blue, then slides aside to expose the next inscription.*

**Worksheet mission**

- `PropIX` — bisect a rectilinear angle

**What students should produce**

Given an angle such as `ABC`, students construct a line from the vertex that divides the angle into two equal angles.

**Answer key**

1. **Why do the equal-radius arcs matter here?**
   They create points on the two arms of the angle that are the same distance from the vertex. That symmetry is what makes the later construction reliable.
2. **How does the construction show that the angle has been split into two equal parts?**
   The intersecting arcs create a point that is equally related to both sides of the angle. Joining that point to the vertex produces two congruent triangles, so the two new angles are equal.
3. **Where can you see an isosceles or equilateral idea hiding inside this construction?**
   The equal radii create equal sides, so the argument depends on isosceles-style equality and congruent triangle reasoning, even if no full equilateral triangle is drawn.

**Success criteria**

- Student draws equal-radius arcs from the angle vertex to mark matching points on each arm.
- Student uses intersecting arcs from those matching points to locate the bisector.
- Student explains that the final line makes two equal angles, not simply a visually centred line.

**Watch for**

- Pupils changing the compass width between the matching arcs.
- Pupils placing the bisector by eye.
- Pupils describing the result as “halfway” without explaining why.

## Chamber 5 — The Stone Lid

**Read-aloud cue**

*At the centre of the vault lies a granite lid banded with green-black bronze. A lifting hook hangs above it, useless until fixed at the exact midpoint of the bar below. Vale runs a hand across the stone and says, “No midpoint, no map.”*

**When the chamber yields**

*The hook takes the strain. Dust falls from the seam. The lid rises just enough for lamplight to catch the edges of the hidden chart beneath.*

**Worksheet mission**

- `PropX` — bisect a finite straight line

**What students should produce**

Given segment `AB`, students construct its midpoint using arcs from the endpoints and identify the perpendicular bisector.

**Answer key**

1. **How do you know the marked midpoint is the same distance from both ends?**
   The midpoint lies on the perpendicular bisector formed from equal-radius arcs drawn from `A` and `B`. Any point on that bisector is equidistant from the endpoints.
2. **Why is the bisecting line also perpendicular to `AB`?**
   The line through the arc intersections is the perpendicular bisector of `AB`. It both meets the segment at its midpoint and forms a right angle with it.
3. **Which arcs prove that the midpoint was constructed rather than guessed?**
   The matching arcs from `A` and `B`, drawn with the same radius above and below the segment, are the proof. Their intersections determine the bisector.

**Success criteria**

- Student draws equal-radius arcs from both endpoints so that they intersect above and below the segment.
- Student joins the arc intersections to form the perpendicular bisector.
- Student identifies the midpoint where that bisector crosses the original segment.

**Watch for**

- Pupils using a radius that is too small for the arcs to meet.
- Pupils marking a middle-looking point without constructing the bisector.
- Pupils naming the midpoint but not the perpendicular relationship.

## Chamber 6 — The Pillar of Right Angles

**Read-aloud cue**

*A crack runs across the ceiling. Loose grit rains down. Ahead, a stone socket waits for a support pillar that must rise from one marked point on the floor line at a perfect right angle. Vale does not look up as she says, “Make it true. The roof will know if you cheat.”*

**When the chamber yields**

*The pillar locks into place with a sharp report. The crack stops spreading. The chamber holds.*

**Worksheet mission**

- `PropXI` — draw a perpendicular at a point on a line

**What students should produce**

From a marked point `P` on a line, students construct a second line through `P` that meets the original line at a right angle.

**Answer key**

1. **Why does this construction create a right angle rather than a line that only looks upright?**
   The construction builds equal lengths on either side of the point, then uses an equilateral-triangle step so that two adjacent angles at `P` are equal. Equal adjacent angles on a straight line are right angles.
2. **Which earlier construction appears again inside this proposition?**
   The equilateral-triangle construction from Proposition I appears again.
3. **How would you convince another explorer that your line is truly perpendicular?**
   Show that the line creates two equal adjacent angles where it meets the straight line. Because angles on a straight line sum to two right angles, equal halves must each be right angles.

**Success criteria**

- Student marks equal lengths on the line on either side of the given point.
- Student uses the equilateral-triangle idea or equivalent arc construction to locate the new point off the line.
- Student justifies perpendicularity by angle equality, not by appearance.

**Watch for**

- Pupils forgetting to centre the construction on the given point.
- Pupils drawing a vertical-looking line without proof.
- Pupils remembering the method but not the reason it creates right angles.

## Chamber 7 — The Escape Shaft

**Read-aloud cue**

*The final lever lies beyond reach, visible through an opening above a sheer wall line. The rescue cable must fall by the shortest route from the ledge point. Vale looks once at the fading light beyond the shaft and says, “This is the last clean drop. Get it wrong and we lose the harbour.”*

**When the chamber yields**

*The cable lands squarely. The release lever slams down. Deep in the vault, the Lens of Alexandria turns on its hidden bearings.*

**Worksheet mission**

- `PropXII` — drop a perpendicular from an external point

**What students should produce**

Given a point such as `P` above a line `AB`, students construct the shortest line from `P` to `AB` by dropping a perpendicular.

**Answer key**

1. **Why is the perpendicular the shortest route from a point to a line?**
   Any slanted segment from the point to the line forms a longer route than the segment that meets the line at a right angle. The perpendicular gives the minimum distance from the point to the line.
2. **What equal distances or equal arcs are used to make the construction reliable?**
   First, a circle centred at the external point creates two points on the line that are equally distant from that point. Then equal-radius arcs from those points locate the perpendicular bisector structure.
3. **How is this mission different from Mission 6, even though both end with a right angle?**
   Mission 6 starts at a fixed point on the line and erects a perpendicular there. Mission 7 starts from a point off the line and finds where the perpendicular lands.

**Success criteria**

- Student begins from the external point, not from an arbitrary point on the line.
- Student creates two equal-distance contact points on the line and uses them to construct the perpendicular.
- Student explains that the result is both perpendicular and the shortest distance to the line.

**Watch for**

- Pupils choosing the foot of the perpendicular by eye.
- Pupils confusing this task with erecting a perpendicular at a chosen point on the line.
- Pupils describing the answer as “straight down” rather than “at right angles”.

## Quick chamber-by-chamber judgement

Use this fast check while circulating:

- **Ready to pass the chamber** — correct construction, visible arcs, clear labels, sound explanation
- **Nearly there** — mostly correct construction, but explanation is thin or one construction mark is missing
- **Needs another attempt** — result is guessed, measured, or cannot be justified from the construction

## In-character feedback lines

Use these if you want the guide to feel more like a live expedition log:

- “The vault accepts that construction.”
- “The lock sees the shape, but it still wants the proof.”
- “Good. The seal turns when the arcs do the talking.”
- “Close, but the chamber does not reward guesswork.”
- “Show Vale why the line must be equal, not merely neat.”
- “That is the right doorway. Now justify why the mechanism should trust it.”

## Suggested debrief prompts

- Which chamber depended most obviously on an earlier chamber?
- Where did equal lengths do the real work?
- Why do visible arcs matter in geometric proof?
- Which chamber felt most like a mechanical routine, and which felt most like reasoning?

## Extension route

After Chamber 7, move naturally into the angle theorems in Playlist 4. Students can treat the completed vault as a map room: the constructions open the chambers, and the angle facts decode the final inscription.
