# The Vault of the Last Geometer

An adventure worksheet for Euclid’s ruler-and-compass constructions.

## Teacher overview

This worksheet turns Playlist 3 in `docs/youtube_metadata.md` into a serial challenge. Students become expedition apprentices helping Professor Helena Vale enter an ancient vault and reset its geometric locks before sunset.

The tone is pulpy and adventurous. The maths stays exact.

Use one mission per lesson, or run the full sequence as a mini-unit. Each mission pairs with one construction video and asks students to reproduce the construction, explain why it works, and unlock the next chamber.

## Learning focus

Students use Euclid’s construction ideas to:

- construct an equilateral triangle on a given line
- copy a line segment from a given point
- cut off a segment equal to a shorter line
- bisect an angle
- bisect a finite straight line
- draw a perpendicular at a point on a line
- drop a perpendicular from an external point to a line

## Materials

- pencil
- ruler or straightedge
- compasses
- eraser
- blank paper for each mission

## How to run it

1. Read the mission briefing aloud.
2. Play the matching video once without stopping.
3. Play it again and pause at key moments.
4. Students complete the construction on blank paper.
5. Students answer the checkpoint questions.
6. When a construction is sound and clearly labelled, award the chamber seal and move on.

## Story hook

At dawn, Professor Helena Vale uncovers the buried entrance to the Vault of the Last Geometer.

Inside lies the Lens of Alexandria, an ancient device that can redirect a destructive beacon away from the harbour. A rival treasure hunter has already disturbed the vault. If the seven geometric locks are not reset before sunset, the beacon will fire and the rescue fleet offshore will be lost.

There is only one way through.

No guessing. No measuring by eye. No forcing the mechanism.

Only Euclid.

## Student briefing

You are a field geometer on Professor Vale’s expedition team.

Your job is to open each chamber using only a ruler and compasses. Every chamber responds to a precise construction. If your reasoning is shaky, the vault stays shut.

### Expedition rules

- Draw lightly first, then darken the final result.
- Label every important point clearly.
- Keep construction arcs visible.
- Be ready to explain why the construction works.
- If you get stuck, return to the video and watch the sequence again.

## Mission 1 — The Triangle Gate

*Three bronze sockets surround the entrance slab. Two are fixed at points `A` and `B`. The third must be placed so all three sides of the locking plate are equal.*

**Watch**

- `PropI` — *Construct an Equilateral Triangle on a Given Line*

**Starter figure**

- Draw a line segment `AB`.

**Your challenge**

Construct an equilateral triangle on the line segment `AB`.

**Checkpoint questions**

1. Why is the new point the same distance from `A` and `B`?
2. How do you know all three sides of the triangle are equal?
3. Which parts of the construction are essential, and which are only scaffolding?

## Mission 2 — The Bridge of Equal Steps

*A narrow bridge spans a floor of pressure tiles. The safe step length is marked on an old bronze rod, but your bridge anchor starts at a different point.*

**Watch**

- `PropII` — *Copy a Line Segment from a Given Point*

**Starter figure**

- Draw a point `P`.
- Draw a separate line segment `CD`.

**Your challenge**

Starting from `P`, construct a new line segment equal in length to `CD`.

**Checkpoint questions**

1. Why can’t you simply measure `CD` with a ruler and mark it off?
2. Which earlier construction idea helps make this one possible?
3. How can you justify that your new segment is exactly equal to `CD`?

## Mission 3 — The Counterweight Cut

*A stone gate rises only if the hanging rope is trimmed to the exact length of a carved key. Too long, and the gate jams. Too short, and the counterweight crashes.*

**Watch**

- `PropIII` — *Cut Off a Segment Equal to a Shorter Line*

**Starter figure**

- Draw a longer line segment `AB`.
- Draw a shorter line segment `CD`.

**Your challenge**

From the longer segment `AB`, cut off a part exactly equal in length to `CD`.

**Checkpoint questions**

1. Where does the equal copied length appear in your finished construction?
2. Why does this proposition depend on being able to copy a segment first?
3. What would go wrong if the marked point were placed by eye?

## Mission 6 — The Pillar of Right Angles

*The ceiling begins to crack. A support pillar must rise from a marked point on the floor line at a perfect right angle or the chamber will collapse.*

**Watch**

- `PropXI` — *Draw a Perpendicular at a Point on a Line*

**Starter figure**

- Draw a line `AB`.
- Mark a point `P` on the line.

**Your challenge**

Construct a line through `P` that is perpendicular to `AB`.

**Checkpoint questions**

1. Why does this construction create a right angle rather than a line that only looks upright?
2. Which earlier construction appears again inside this proposition?
3. How would you convince another explorer that your line is truly perpendicular?

## Mission 7 — The Escape Shaft

*The release lever is visible from an upper ledge, but the rescue cable must drop to the wall line by the shortest possible route.*

**Watch**

- `PropXII` — *Drop a Perpendicular from an External Point*

**Starter figure**

- Draw a line `AB`.
- Mark a point `P` above the line.

**Your challenge**

Construct the perpendicular from `P` to line `AB`.

**Checkpoint questions**

1. Why is the perpendicular the shortest route from a point to a line?
2. What equal distances or equal arcs are used to make the construction reliable?
3. How is this mission different from Mission 6, even though both end with a right angle?

## Final chamber — Reset the Lens

Professor Vale fits the seven seals into the final mechanism. The beacon turns. The harbour is safe. The rival treasure hunter arrives too late and finds only a locked stone door and seven perfect constructions.

Your expedition log is now complete.

## Debrief questions

1. Which mission felt easiest, and why?
2. Which mission felt most like a puzzle rather than a recipe?
3. Which constructions depended on earlier ones?
4. Where did precision matter most in the story?
5. If you were designing Mission 8, what would the vault demand next?

## Extension ideas

- Ask students to invent an eighth chamber using one of the same constructions in a new setting.
- Ask students to annotate one finished construction with a short proof of why it works.
- Move into Playlist 4 and treat the angle theorems as the code needed to read the map recovered from the vault.

## Teacher note on tone

If you want the adventure to feel more like a full campaign, keep a visible “vault progress” display on the board and award one chamber seal per completed construction. If you want a lighter touch, drop the seals and simply keep the mission titles and story beats.
